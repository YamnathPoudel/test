Pascal's Law & Hydraulic Press — Theory

📐 Pascal's Law

Pascal's Law states that pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted equally in all directions throughout the fluid and acts with equal force on equal areas. Stated by Blaise Pascal (1653), it forms the theoretical basis for all hydraulic machines.

🔧 Working of Hydraulic Press

A hydraulic press consists of two pistons of different cross-sectional areas connected through an incompressible fluid. A small input force on the smaller piston creates a pressure that is transmitted to the larger piston, producing a multiplied output force.

📊 Mathematical Relationship

Pressure is defined as force per unit area: P = F / A. Since pressure is equal throughout the enclosed fluid (Pascal's Law): P = F₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂. Therefore the output force is: F₂ = F₁ × (A₂/A₁). The ratio A₂/A₁ is the mechanical advantage.

⚡ Applications

Hydraulic presses are used in: car jacks, hydraulic brakes, cranes, injection moulding machines, hydraulic lifts, metal stamping, dental chairs, aircraft control systems, and industrial compactors. All exploit Pascal's law for force multiplication.

Principle of Hydraulic Press: A large force is developed on the larger piston when a small force is applied on the smaller piston, because the pressure created in the fluid is transmitted equally to every point in the enclosed fluid system.
